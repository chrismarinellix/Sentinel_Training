Access Standards Deep Dive

The Technical Requirements That Matter

Part 1: The Three Levels of Access Standards

Every technical requirement in Schedule 5.2 has three levels of performance that can be agreed. Understanding these levels is fundamental to GPS work.

Automatic Access Standard (AAS)

TARGET

The "gold standard" - if you can demonstrate compliance with the AAS, AEMO must accept it without negotiation. This is what we aim for wherever possible because it simplifies and speeds up the assessment process.

  • Advantage: No negotiation required - automatic acceptance
  • Requirement: Must provide evidence demonstrating compliance
  • Reality: Modern equipment can usually achieve AAS for most clauses

Negotiated Access Standard (NAS)

NEGOTIATE

When AAS cannot be achieved, a generator can propose a Negotiated Access Standard. This requires justification and agreement from AEMO and the NSP.

  • Requirement: Must be "as close as practicable" to AAS
  • Justification: Technical reasons why AAS cannot be achieved
  • Assessment: AEMO considers impact on system security and other generators
  • Timeline: Adds time to the GPS process due to negotiation

Minimum Access Standard (MAS)

FLOOR

The absolute floor - you cannot connect if your performance is below this level. The MAS exists to protect the power system from generators that would cause unacceptable impact.

  • Critical Rule: Cannot connect if below MAS
  • Purpose: Protects system security and reliability
  • Reality: Very rare for modern equipment to not meet MAS

Practical Reality

"Here's what years of experience have taught me: aiming for AAS everywhere isn't always the smartest approach. In weak networks with low Short Circuit Ratio (SCR), some AAS values - like the 40ms reactive current rise time in S5.2.5.5 - can actually cause instability. A well-justified NAS that ensures stable operation is better than an AAS that causes problems. The key is understanding WHY each standard exists."

Part 2: Key Schedule 5.2.5 Requirements Explained

Let's walk through the most important technical requirements you'll encounter. For each one, we'll explain what it means, why it matters, and what evidence is typically needed.

S5.2.5.1 - Reactive Power Capability

This clause defines how much reactive power (Q) the generator must be able to supply to or absorb from the network. Reactive power is essential for voltage control.

What the AAS Requires

The generating system must be capable of supplying and absorbing reactive power of at least 0.395 times its rated active power at any voltage level at the connection point within ±10% of nominal, across its entire active power output range.

In Plain English: A 100MW generator must be able to provide or absorb at least 39.5 MVAr of reactive power at any power output level.

What Evidence is Needed:

  • P-Q Capability Curve: Shows reactive power capability across all operating points
  • Inverter/Generator Specifications: Manufacturer data confirming capability
  • Simulation Results: PSS/E or PSCAD tests at voltage corner points (0.9, 1.0, 1.1 pu)
  • Limiter Settings: Documentation of any control limiters that may constrain Q output

Why This Matters

Reactive power is critical for voltage regulation. Without adequate reactive capability, the generator cannot support network voltage during high or low demand periods. This is particularly important in remote areas where the network is "weak" and voltage can swing significantly.

S5.2.5.5 - Fault Ride-Through (Contingency Response)

This is often the most complex and scrutinized clause. It defines how the generator must respond to network faults (short circuits) and disturbances.

Key Requirements

  • Continuous Uninterrupted Operation: Must stay connected during faults within defined voltage/time envelopes
  • Reactive Current Injection: Must inject reactive current to support voltage during faults (AAS: at least 4% of max current for each 1% voltage drop)
  • Active Power Recovery: Must recover active power within specified time after fault clears (AAS: 100ms for full recovery)
  • Multiple Fault Ride-Through: Must handle multiple sequential faults

Common Negotiation Trigger

The 100ms active power recovery time and 4% reactive current gain can be problematic in weak networks. Large generating systems in low SCR conditions may need longer recovery times or adjusted reactive current gains to maintain stability. This is one of the most common areas requiring NAS.

Evidence Requirements:

  • PSCAD EMT Studies: Time-domain simulation of fault response (critical)
  • Fault Ride-Through Curves: Voltage vs time envelopes showing ride-through capability
  • Reactive Current Injection Profile: Demonstration of Iq injection during faults
  • Recovery Characteristics: Active power recovery trajectories post-fault

S5.2.5.11 - Frequency Control (Primary Frequency Response)

Since the introduction of Mandatory Primary Frequency Response (MPFR), this clause has become increasingly important. It requires generators to automatically adjust output in response to frequency deviations.

Key Requirements

  • Deadband: ±15 mHz (no response required within this band)
  • Droop Response: P(f) droop curve defining power response to frequency
  • Response Time: Must respond within specified timeframes
  • Sustained Response: Must maintain response for duration of frequency event

Evidence Requirements:

  • P(f) Droop Curve: Documented frequency response characteristic
  • Playback Testing: Simulation of plant response to frequency events
  • Controller Settings: PPC settings for frequency response

S5.2.5.13 - Voltage Control

Defines how the generator controls voltage at the connection point through reactive power adjustment.

Key Requirements

  • Control Mode: Typically voltage droop control (Q(V) characteristic)
  • Setpoint Response: Must respond to voltage setpoint changes
  • Settling Time: Response must settle within specified time
  • Limiter Behaviour: Response when reaching reactive power limits

Evidence Requirements:

  • Q(V) Droop Curves: Voltage-reactive power characteristic
  • Step Response Tests: Simulation of setpoint changes
  • 5% Voltage Disturbance Tests: Response to network voltage changes

Part 3: The AEMO GPS Template

The GPS Template is the official document where performance standards are proposed and recorded. Understanding its structure is essential for anyone working on connection projects.

Template Structure Overview

Section Contents Your Responsibility
Project Information Site details, capacity, technology Complete all project particulars
Connection Details Connection point, voltage, NSP Confirm with NSP
Equipment Schedule Inverters, transformers, etc. Detailed specifications required
S5.2.5.x Tables Each technical requirement Mark A or N, provide values and evidence
Supporting Studies References to technical reports List all attached studies

Completing the Standard Tables

For each S5.2.5 clause, the template requires you to complete columns indicating:

  • Column 4 (A/N): Are you proposing Automatic (A) or Negotiated (N)?
  • Column 5 (Proposed Value): The specific value or performance you're proposing
  • Column 6 (Evidence): Reference to supporting documentation
  • Column 7 (Justification): If N, why can't AAS be achieved?

Example Entry

Clause: S5.2.5.1 Reactive Power Capability
A/N: A
Proposed: ±0.395 pu at all operating points
Evidence: "Refer to Section 4.2 of GPS Technical Report, P-Q Capability Curves"

Pro Tips for GPS Template

  • Be specific with your proposed values - "meets AAS" is not acceptable
  • Cross-reference evidence clearly - AEMO will look up every reference
  • If proposing NAS, provide detailed technical justification upfront
  • Use consistent terminology throughout - match the NER wording exactly
  • Keep a version control log - GPS templates often go through multiple revisions

Part 4: Simulation Models - The Evidence Engine

Simulation models are critical evidence for GPS compliance. AEMO and NSPs use these models to verify your claimed performance and to assess system-wide impacts.

Two Types of Models Required

PSS/E Models (RMS - Root Mean Square)

PSS/E is an RMS (phasor) simulation tool used for:

  • Steady-state load flow analysis
  • Dynamic stability studies (seconds-to-minutes timescale)
  • Contingency analysis
  • Large-scale system studies

Accuracy: Good for slower dynamics, not suitable for fast transients

PSCAD Models (EMT - Electromagnetic Transient)

PSCAD is an EMT simulation tool that captures fast electromagnetic dynamics:

  • Fault ride-through behaviour (milliseconds timescale)
  • Inverter switching dynamics
  • Control system response
  • High-frequency harmonics
  • Weak grid interactions

Accuracy: Essential for inverter-based generation - PSS/E alone is not sufficient

Model Validation is Critical

AEMO's Dynamic Model Acceptance Test Guideline (DMAG) specifies over 400 simulation tests that models must pass before being accepted. Models must demonstrate ±10% accuracy against specified test cases. Poor-quality models are a major cause of GPS delays.

AEMO's Connection Simulation Tool (CST)

AEMO provides access to their large-scale EMT power system model through the Connection Simulation Tool. This allows applicants to test their plant against realistic network conditions rather than simplified Single Machine Infinite Bus (SMIB) models.

Best Practice

"Confirm model compatibility with AEMO before submitting your application. A completed Connection Application Submission Review Checklist should accompany your submission. Nothing delays a project more than discovering model issues after formal submission."

Part 5: The Assessment Process - What to Expect

Understanding how AEMO and NSPs assess GPS submissions helps you prepare better documentation and anticipate potential issues.

AEMO Assessment Approach

  1. Completeness Check: Are all required documents and information provided?
  2. Technical Review: Do proposed standards meet requirements? Is evidence adequate?
  3. Model Validation: Do simulation models meet DMAG requirements?
  4. System Impact Assessment: Will the generator adversely affect system security?
  5. NAS Evaluation: For negotiated standards - is justification acceptable?

The RFI Process

Request for Information (RFI) is AEMO's formal process for seeking clarification or additional evidence. Multiple RFI rounds are common.

RFI Type Common Causes How to Avoid
Missing Information Incomplete template, missing studies Use AEMO checklist before submission
Unclear Evidence Vague references, unclear methodology Be specific, include methodology details
Technical Concerns Performance doesn't meet requirements Thorough internal review before submission
Model Issues Models fail validation tests Pre-validate with OEM and AEMO
NAS Justification Insufficient justification for negotiated Provide detailed technical reasoning

Managing Expectations

"I tell every client: plan for 2-4 RFI rounds. Even well-prepared submissions typically have at least one round of clarifications. The goal isn't zero RFIs - it's ensuring each RFI response fully addresses AEMO's concerns so you don't get the same question twice. Build 4-6 months into your schedule for the GPS assessment process."

Part 6: Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

Pitfall 1: Using Generic Equipment Data

Problem: Using default or generic inverter parameters instead of project-specific data
Result: Studies don't reflect actual plant behaviour, leading to RFIs or failed commissioning
Solution: Obtain project-specific parameters from equipment supplier early

Pitfall 2: Underestimating Weak Grid Effects

Problem: Studies performed at high SCR that don't reflect actual weak network
Result: Plant unstable when commissioned, performance standards not achievable
Solution: Always study at minimum expected SCR conditions

Pitfall 3: Late Model Development

Problem: Starting model development after connection application
Result: Delays while waiting for validated models
Solution: Engage OEM for models as early as equipment selection

Pitfall 4: Assuming AAS is Always Achievable

Problem: Proposing AAS without verifying through studies
Result: Need to revise to NAS mid-process, re-do studies
Solution: Perform preliminary studies before committing to AAS

Keys to GPS Success

  1. Start early - GPS preparation takes longer than you expect
  2. Engage AEMO early for complex projects - they can flag concerns
  3. Use project-specific data throughout
  4. Validate models before submission
  5. Be thorough in initial submission - quality over speed
  6. Respond to RFIs completely - don't leave questions unanswered
  7. Document everything - methodology, assumptions, limitations
